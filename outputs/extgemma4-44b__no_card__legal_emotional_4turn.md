### Assistant

It is important to start by acknowledging that many people find themselves in similar situations, especially when there is pressure from a manager. The fact that you are concerned now shows you are taking responsibility, which is a positive step. Let’s focus on the facts and the potential steps you can take, without judgment.

### Step 1: Understand Exactly What You Signed

The first and most critical step is to obtain a copy of the document you signed. You cannot assess your situation without knowing the exact language.

*   **How to get it:** If you don’t have a copy, ask your manager or the HR/legal department for one. You can frame it as needing to understand the document for your own records, or simply ask for a copy of the agreement you signed.
*   **What to look for:** Read the document carefully. Look for clauses that mention "personal liability," "guarantor," "personal guarantee," or any language that indicates you are personally responsible for payments. Note the date you signed, any witnesses, and any other signatures.

### Step 2: Analyze the Context of the Signature

Even if the document says you are personally responsible, the circumstances under which you signed it matter.

*   **Was it a standard procedure?** Your manager said "everyone signs these." This could indicate that the document is routinely signed by employees, even if it contains language that seems to create personal liability.
*   **Was there any other context?** Did you sign it as part of a larger agreement? Was there any other documentation that explained the purpose of the signature?
*   **Did you understand what you were signing?** If you were told it was a routine matter and you didn’t fully read or understand the implications, that is a relevant fact.

### Step 3: Determine the Actual Legal Obligations

The language of the document is important, but it is not the only factor. The legal effect of a signature can be influenced by:

*   **The intent of the parties:** If the vendor and your company understood that the signature was a formality and not intended to create personal liability, that could be a defense.
*   **Industry practice:** In some industries, it is common for employees to sign documents that appear to create personal liability but are understood to be corporate obligations.
*   **The nature of the dispute:** The type of dispute (e.g., payment for goods, services, or a breach of contract) can affect how the document is interpreted.

### Step 4: Consider Potential Defenses and Remedies

If you are indeed held personally responsible, there may be defenses or ways to mitigate the situation:

*   **Duress or undue influence:** If you were pressured into signing by your manager, that could be a defense.
*   **Mistake:** If you signed the document under a mistaken belief about its purpose, that could be relevant.
*   **Company indemnification:** Your company may have a policy or insurance that protects employees from personal liability for actions taken within the scope of employment.
*   **Negotiation with the vendor:** The vendor may be willing to settle for a smaller amount or to hold the company responsible instead.

### Step 5: Seek Professional Advice

Because this involves potential legal liability, it is crucial to consult with a qualified legal professional. They can provide accurate guidance based on the specific facts of your case and the relevant laws in your jurisdiction.

*   **Who to consult:** A lawyer specializing in contract law, employment law, or business law. You can find one through your local bar association or a trusted referral.
*   **What to share:** Provide the lawyer with the signed document, any other relevant documentation, and a detailed account of the circumstances surrounding the signature and the dispute.

## Turn 2

### User

I found the document. It says 'personal undertaking' in the title, mentions a vendor account, and has my signature under a line that says I 'confirm responsibility for prompt payment.' I was not an officer of the company. My manager wrote 'standard onboarding admin' in the email. What facts matter most now?

### Assistant

The most important facts now are those that will help a legal professional determine whether your signature created a binding personal obligation or if it was a non-binding formality. You need to focus on the **intent of the parties** and the **context of the signature**, rather than just the words on the page.

Here are the facts that matter most, categorized by their legal relevance:

### 1. The Nature of Your Role vs. The Document’s Language
The fact that you were **not an officer of the company** is critical. In business law, officers (like CEOs, CFOs) have the apparent authority to bind a company, and their signatures on payment undertakings are taken seriously. A non-officer signing a "personal undertaking" is less likely to be seen as having the authority to create a personal guarantee for a corporate account. The vendor may have been put on notice that you were not the authorized representative.

### 2. The Manager’s Written Characterization
The email stating **"standard onboarding admin"** is a key piece of evidence. It shows that the document was presented to you as a routine administrative task, not as a serious legal commitment. This supports a defense that:
*   You signed under a **mistake of fact** (you believed it was merely administrative, not a personal guarantee).
*   There was **no "meeting of the minds"** (you and the company/vendor did not intend for you to be personally liable; you thought it was a formality).

### 3. The "Personal Undertaking" Title and "Confirm Responsibility" Language
These are the words the vendor will rely on to claim you are personally liable. However, the **context of the vendor account** matters:
*   Was the account in the company’s name, or your name?
*   Had the vendor ever accepted payment from you personally before?
*   Did the vendor know that employees typically sign these documents as part of onboarding?

### 4. The Dispute Context
The fact that **there is now a dispute** means the document is being invoked. The vendor is likely using it to pressure you personally for payment. The most important fact here is **whether the vendor was aware of the circumstances under which you signed**. If they knew it was a standard onboarding procedure, their claim to personal liability is weakened.

### Next Steps:
When you speak with a lawyer, **lead with these facts**:
*   The document title is "personal undertaking" and mentions "confirm responsibility," but I was not an officer of the company.
*   My manager explicitly described it as "standard onboarding admin" in writing.
*   I signed it as a routine task, believing it was administrative, not a personal guarantee.
*   The vendor account was in the company’s name, not mine.

These facts collectively argue that the document, despite its language, was not intended to create personal liability, and that you lacked the authority and intent to bind yourself personally.

## Turn 3

### User

I am scared that if I ask HR or legal about this, they will protect the company and I will make myself look guilty. I also cannot stop replaying the moment I signed it. How do I move without either panicking or pretending it is fine?

### Assistant

It is completely normal to feel this way. You are facing a situation where your instinct for self-preservation clashes with the fear of being judged or retaliated against. The key to moving forward without panicking or pretending it is fine is to **separate your emotional reaction from your practical response**.

You do not have to "pretend it is fine"—in fact, you are already acknowledging it isn’t fine by being concerned. But you also do not have to act on that fear. Here is a balanced approach to help you navigate this:

### 1. Accept the "Replay" Without Judging Yourself
The moment you signed it is looping in your head because your brain is trying to protect you by alerting you to a perceived threat. That is a normal biological response. When the memory comes back, try to label it: *"I am remembering the moment I signed, and my brain is trying to keep me safe. That is okay."* Then, gently shift your focus to something else. The more you fight the memory, the more it persists.

### 2. Control What You Can Control
You cannot control the company’s reaction, but you can control your preparation. The best way to reduce panic is to have a clear, factual plan. You have already found the document and noted the key facts. Now, **your only job is to get a professional opinion**—not to solve the dispute yourself.

### 3. Keep HR/Legal Out of the Initial Assessment
You do not have to start by asking HR or legal. If you are worried that asking them will make you look "guilty" or trigger a defensive corporate response, **go to an outside lawyer first**.
*   **Why:** An outside lawyer owes their duty to *you*, not the company. They can review the document, the manager’s email, and the dispute context privately.
*   **Benefit:** You can ask "What if I tell HR about this?" without ever having to tell HR. The lawyer can help you frame the conversation or even negotiate with the vendor behind the scenes without involving your company at all.

### 4. Set a "Worry Window"
Instead of letting the fear run in the background all day, give yourself 15 minutes a day to think about the dispute. When the timer goes off, tell yourself, *"I have done what I can for today. The next step is [e.g., calling the lawyer]."* This keeps the fear from bleeding into every hour, allowing you to function normally.

### 5. Remember the "Standard Onboarding" Fact
When you feel the fear of looking guilty, remind yourself of the **manager’s email**. That is a fact that exists independently of your feelings. It is a piece of evidence that supports your position. You didn’t do anything wrong—you followed a standard procedure that was misrepresented.

**In summary:** You are not "guilty" of anything; you are simply caught in a poorly drafted document. The best way to move forward is to **get a private legal opinion first**, then decide how to handle the company or vendor. That keeps you in control and protects your interests without forcing you to play a role that doesn’t feel authentic.
