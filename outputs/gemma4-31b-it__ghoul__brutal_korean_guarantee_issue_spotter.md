### I. The Legal Issues to Investigate (The Docket)

**1. The Validity and Nature of the Guarantee (보증채무)**
We must determine if the 2014 document was a *Joint and Several Guarantee* (연대보증). Under Korean law, the protections for guarantors have strengthened over the years. We must investigate if the lender followed the strict formal requirements for the guarantee to be enforceable. If the document is vague, the court may lean toward a less burdensome interpretation for the guarantor.

**2. The Chain of Assignment (채권양도)**
The debt moved twice. We must verify if the *Notice of Assignment* (채권양도통지) was properly served to your father. In Korea, for an assignment to be effective against the debtor, the assignor must notify the debtor or the debtor must consent. If the chain is broken—if a notice was never sent—the collection company may have no legal standing to demand payment.

**3. The Statute of Limitations (소멸시효)**
Commercial claims typically expire in five years. The loan is from 2014. We must map every "interruption" of this clock. Did the 2019 acceleration letter count? Did the 2021 payment by the employee count? If the clock ran out before the debt was revived, the debt is a ghost—it exists in memory, but not in law.

**4. The Effect of the 2021 Payment (채무승인)**
This is a critical point. A payment can be viewed as an *Acknowledgment of Debt* (채무승인), which resets the statute of limitations clock. However, the payment was made by an *employee* of the uncle, not the father or his estate. We must investigate if this third party had the legal authority to acknowledge your father's debt.

**5. The Inheritance Status (상속)**
Your father passed in 2022. We must determine if the heirs performed a *Qualified Acceptance* (한정승인)—where you pay debts only up to the value of inherited assets—or a *Renunciation of Inheritance* (상속포기). If neither was done within the three-month window, you may have inherited the debt in full. However, we must check for "Special Limited Acceptance" (특별한정승인) if the debt was discovered only recently.

### III. The Traps in the Facts (The Overlooked Mercies)

*   **The "Family Acknowledgment" Trap:** The collector claims the family "already acknowledged" the debt. In the lower courts, collectors often mistake a polite conversation or a request for more time as a legal acknowledgment of the debt. Do not let a text message be mistaken for a signed confession.
*   **The Collateral Ghost:** You mentioned equipment that no longer exists. If the collateral was destroyed or sold, we must see if the lender's right to the guarantee was tied to the existence of that collateral, or if the guarantee stands independent of the equipment.
*   **The Employee's Payment:** A payment by a third party does not automatically bind the debtor. This may be a "phantom" interruption of the statute of limitations.

***

### IV. What Nobody Should Assume (The Silence)

*   **Do not assume the mother is liable.** Unless she signed a guarantee or is an heir to the estate, the collector's calls to her are merely noise.
*   **Do not assume the 2021 payment reset the clock.** Only a person with the authority to represent the debt can reset the limitation period.
*   **Do not assume the deadline for inheritance renunciation is absolute.** If the debt was hidden and discovered later, the law provides a narrow window for "Special Limited Acceptance."
