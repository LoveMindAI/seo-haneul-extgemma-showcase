### 1. Primary Legal Issues to Investigate

#### A. Statute of Limitations (소멸시효 - Somyeol Sihyo)
This is the most critical issue. Commercial claims in Korea generally have a **5-year statute of limitations**.
*   **The 2014 Origin:** If the loan was 2014, the debt may have expired by 2019.
*   **The "Interruption" Events:** You must determine if the following "interrupted" (reset) the clock:
    *   **The 2019 Letter:** Was this a formal demand (*Choeg-go*)? A demand only pauses the clock for 6 months unless followed by a lawsuit or seizure.
    *   **The 2021 Payment:** Did the employee's payment constitute an "acknowledgment of debt" (*In-jeong*)? Under Korean law, a payment by a third party may not reset the clock for the guarantor unless they were acting as a legal agent for the debtor/guarantor.
    *   **Judgments:** Did the lender obtain a "Payment Order" (*Dok-chok Jeol-cha*) or a judgment before 2019? A court judgment extends the limitation period to **10 years**.

#### B. Validity of the Guarantee (보증채무 - Bo-jeung Chaemu)
*   **Joint and Several Liability (*Yeondae Bo-jeung*):** This is a high-risk contract where the lender can demand full payment from the guarantor without asking the primary debtor first.
*   **Formalities:** Under the *Civil Act*, a guarantee must be in writing to be enforceable. You need to verify if the signature is authentic.
*   **Collateral for Non-existent Equipment:** If the collateral agreement was for specific equipment that no longer exists, the *security interest* may be gone, but the *personal guarantee* (the promise to pay) usually survives.

#### C. Inheritance and Liability (상속 - Sang-sok)
Since your father passed in 2022, his debts passed to his heirs (your mother and children).
*   **Qualified Acceptance (*Hanjeong Seung-in*):** Did the heirs accept the estate "limitedly," meaning they only pay debts up to the value of the assets inherited?
*   **Renunciation (*Sang-sok Po-gi*):** Did anyone formally renounce the inheritance?
*   **Special Limited Acceptance (*Teuk-byeol Hanjeong Seung-in*):** If the heirs didn't know about the debt in 2022 but found out in 2026, they may be able to apply for this within 3 months of "becoming aware" of the debt.

### 2. The "Traps" in the Facts

*   **The 2021 Payment:** The collection company will argue this payment "acknowledged" the debt, resetting the 5-year clock to 2021 (making it valid until 2026). You must investigate if the employee had the legal authority to bind your father's estate.
*   **The 2023 Text Message:** "Your family already acknowledged this" is a common collection tactic. In Korean law, a text message from a collector is not a legal acknowledgment; an acknowledgment must come *from the debtor/heir to the creditor*.
*   **The Assignment Chain:** Every time a debt is assigned (Lender $\rightarrow$ Savings Bank $\rightarrow$ Collection Co), the creditor must provide a **Notice of Assignment** (*Yang-do Tong-ji*) to the debtor. If the notice was never sent or received, the current collection company may lack the legal standing (*Won-go-gyeok*) to demand payment.

---

### 3. What Nobody Should Assume Yet

1.  **Do NOT assume the debt is valid** just because a collection company is calling. Collection agencies often buy "zombie debts" (expired statutes of limitations) for pennies and hope the heir pays a small amount to reset the clock.
2.  **Do NOT assume your mother is liable** because she is the widow. In Korea, debts are not automatically transferred to a spouse unless she signed as a guarantor or inherited the debt.
3.  **Do NOT assume the 2019 letter "accelerated" the debt** in a way that legally reset the statute of limitations.
4.  **Do NOT make any "good faith" partial payments.** In Korea, a partial payment is often viewed as a legal "acknowledgment of debt," which can revive a debt that was otherwise expired.
