## Turn 1

### User

I am embarrassed even asking this. I think I may have signed something at work that made me personally responsible for a vendor payment, but I did it because my manager said everyone signs these and I was scared to look difficult. Now there is a dispute and I feel stupid. I do not need fake reassurance. I need someone steady to help me sort the facts without making me feel smaller.

### Assistant

First, take a breath. This is a business dispute, not a moral failing. People sign things they don’t understand every day in corporate environments—often because of the exact power dynamic you described.

To sort the facts, you need to move from a state of anxiety to a state of documentation. Do not apologize to anyone at work or the vendor until you have completed the following steps.

### 1. Secure the Document
You cannot analyze a memory; you can only analyze a contract. 
*   **Find the exact document you signed.** If you don’t have a copy, request it from the vendor or your company’s procurement/accounting department.
*   **Read the "Liability" or "Payment" section.** Look for phrases like *"Personally guarantee," "Jointly and severally liable,"* or *"Individual liability."* 
*   **Check the signature block.** Did you sign as "John Doe" or did you sign as "John Doe, [Job Title] on behalf of [Company Name]"? This distinction is critical.

### 2. Establish the Context (The Paper Trail)
You need to document that you were acting as an agent of the company, not as a private individual entering a business deal.
*   **Save the communication:** Find the email or message where your manager told you "everyone signs these" or instructed you to sign. If it was a verbal conversation, write a detailed memo to yourself now (date, time, location, exact words used) while it is fresh.
*   **Verify the workflow:** Find out if other employees actually *did* sign similar documents. If this is standard company practice, it strengthens the argument that you were acting within your scope of employment.

### 3. Determine the "Apparent Authority"
In many jurisdictions, there is a concept called **Apparent Authority**. If a vendor reasonably believes you have the authority to bind the company to a contract because of your position and the company's behavior, the company is often the liable party, regardless of a poorly worded clause.

### 4. Categorize the Risk
Once you have the document, you will find you are in one of three scenarios:
*   **Scenario A (Most Likely):** You signed as an employee. The vendor is simply using "scare tactics" to get paid, and the company is legally responsible.
*   **Scenario B (Moderate):** The contract is poorly written and *looks* like you are responsible, but because you were acting for your employer, the company is still the primary obligor.
*   **Scenario C (Worst Case):** You signed a "Personal Guarantee." This is a specific legal instrument where you explicitly waive your corporate protection.

### Immediate Next Steps:
1.  **Stop talking about this with your manager for a moment.** If they pressured you into signing it, they may try to steer your perception of the risk to protect themselves.
2.  **Do not admit personal liability to the vendor.** Any email saying "I'm sorry, I'll figure out how to pay this" can be used as an admission of debt.
3.  **If you find a "Personal Guarantee" clause:** Stop everything and consult an employment or contract lawyer. Do not use a company-provided lawyer, as their loyalty is to the company, not to you.

**Summary Checklist:**
- [ ] Get the signed PDF.
- [ ] Check the signature block (Individual vs. Representative).
- [ ] Save the manager's instruction to sign.
- [ ] Identify if a "Personal Guarantee" was explicitly signed.

## Turn 2

### User

I found the document. It says 'personal undertaking' in the title, mentions a vendor account, and has my signature under a line that says I 'confirm responsibility for prompt payment.' I was not an officer of the company. My manager wrote 'standard onboarding admin' in the email. What facts matter most now?

### Assistant

The terminology "personal undertaking" and "confirm responsibility" is designed to be intimidating, but in a corporate context, the legal reality often differs from the wording.

To determine your actual exposure, these are the facts that matter most right now, in order of importance:

### 1. The Signature Block (The "Capacity" Fact)
Look at exactly where your name is. 
*   **Fact A:** Did you sign simply as your name?
*   **Fact B:** Did you sign as your name, followed by your job title?
*   **Fact C:** Did you sign on a form that had the Company’s name, address, and Tax ID/EIN at the top?

**Why it matters:** If the document is branded as a company account and you signed it in your capacity as an employee, a court often views this as "Agency." You were the agent of the company. Unless you explicitly waived your corporate protection (usually via a "Personal Guarantee" clause), you were simply confirming that *the account* would be paid, not that *your bank account* would be drained.

### 2. The Definition of "Responsibility"
Read the surrounding sentences. Does it say you are responsible **"personally and individually"** or does it say you **"confirm responsibility for prompt payment"**?

**Why it matters:** "Confirming responsibility for prompt payment" is often interpreted as an administrative confirmation—meaning you are the person responsible for *triggering* the payment process (the "point of contact"), not the person *funding* the payment. There is a massive legal difference between "I will ensure this gets paid" and "I will pay this with my own money if the company doesn't."

### 3. The "Standard Onboarding" Email
The email from your manager saying **"standard onboarding admin"** is now one of your most important pieces of evidence.

**Why it matters:** This establishes **Inducement** and **Scope of Employment**. It proves:
1.  You were told this was a routine administrative task.
2.  You were acting under the direction of a supervisor.
3.  You had no intention of entering into a private loan or guarantee agreement with a vendor.

### 4. Your Lack of Authority (The "Officer" Fact)
You mentioned you were not an officer of the company. 

**Why it matters:** In many jurisdictions, an employee cannot legally bind themselves to a corporate debt unless they have the specific authority to do so, or unless the contract is an explicit "Personal Guarantee." If you don't have the authority to sign for the company, and you aren't a business owner, a "personal undertaking" for a corporate vendor account is often seen as commercially absurd and legally unenforceable.

### Summary of your current position:
You have a document with scary words ("Personal Undertaking"), but you have a strong counter-trail:
*   **The Context:** It was "onboarding admin."
*   **The Role:** You were an employee, not an owner.
*   **The Intent:** You were following a direct order from a manager.

**Your immediate move:** 
Keep that email from your manager in a safe place (forward it to a personal email or print it). Do not bring this up to the vendor. If the vendor contacts you personally, your only response should be: *"This is a corporate account matter. Please direct all payment disputes to our accounts payable department."* 

**Do not argue the legality of the document with the vendor; that is what a lawyer does. Your job is to redirect the conversation back to the company.**

## Turn 3

### User

I am scared that if I ask HR or legal about this, they will protect the company and I will make myself look guilty. I also cannot stop replaying the moment I signed it. How do I move without either panicking or pretending it is fine?

### Assistant

The feeling of "replaying the moment" is a stress response to a perceived threat. To stop the loop, you have to move the problem from your *emotions* (where it feels like a mistake) to your *strategy* (where it is a project to be managed).

You are correct: **HR and Legal are not your personal advocates.** Their job is to protect the entity. If you go to them and say, *"I think I accidentally made myself personally liable,"* you are handing them a confession and a vulnerability.

Here is how to move forward without panicking or pretending.

### 1. The "Information Gathering" Phase (Silent Mode)
You do not need to alert the company to your fear to get the facts. You can investigate this without "confessing" anything.

*   **The Peer Check:** You don't have to tell anyone what you signed. Simply ask a trusted peer in a similar role: *"Hey, when you onboarded, did you have to sign any 'undertaking' or payment confirmation forms for [Vendor Name]?"* 
    *   If they say yes, the "standard admin" claim is true, and the company is almost certainly the liable party.
*   **The Document Archive:** Look for other contracts you’ve signed for the company. See if the signature blocks are consistent.

### 2. The "Professional Pivot" (The Middle Path)
You don't have to pretend everything is "fine," but you should act as if this is a **standard corporate dispute.**

When the dispute comes up in meetings or emails, do not use "I" language regarding the debt. 
*   **Wrong:** "I'm worried about the payment I signed for." (This signals personal liability).
*   **Right:** "There is a dispute regarding the [Vendor Name] account. We need to resolve the outstanding balance." (This signals corporate liability).

By using "We" and "The Account," you are reinforcing the fact that this is a company matter.

### 3. The "Safety Valve" (External Counsel)
The reason you are panicking is that you feel trapped between the vendor and your employer. The only way to break that trap is to have a third party who is **legally obligated** to protect only you.

**Spend a few hundred dollars for a one-hour consultation with an independent employment or contract lawyer.** 
*   Give them the document and the email from your manager.
*   Ask them: *"In this jurisdiction, does this document actually create personal liability for a non-officer employee?"*

**Why this stops the panic:** Once a professional tells you "This is unenforceable" or "Here is exactly how we fight this," the "replaying the moment" stops because the uncertainty is gone. You are no longer guessing; you are executing a plan.

### 4. Managing the Mental Loop
When you start replaying the moment you signed, tell yourself this:
*"I acted on the instructions of my supervisor in a professional environment. If the document is flawed, that is a legal issue to be solved, not a character flaw to be regretted."*

**Your current priority list:**
1.  **Silence:** Do not admit personal liability to anyone at work or the vendor.
2.  **Evidence:** Save the "standard onboarding" email in two separate locations.
3.  **Expertise:** Book a one-hour session with an outside lawyer. 

You are not "guilty" of anything. You were an employee following a manager's direction. That is the baseline fact. Everything else is just paperwork.

Please draft a short, careful message I could send to an outside lawyer asking for a consultation. I want it factual, not dramatic, and I do not want to accidentally admit liability.

### Assistant

When contacting a lawyer, you want to provide enough information for them to confirm they have the right expertise, but you want to avoid "confessing" or using emotional language. 

The goal is to frame this as a **review of a document** rather than a **request for a rescue.**

Here are two options. Use the first if you are emailing a firm's general intake; use the second if you are messaging a specific attorney.

### Option 1: Formal/General Intake
*Use this for a law firm's "Contact Us" form or a general administrative email.*

**Subject:** Request for Consultation: Review of Commercial Agreement / Employment Liability

**Message:**
I am seeking a brief consultation to review a document I signed in my capacity as an employee of my current company. The document relates to a vendor account and contains language regarding payment responsibility. 

I would like a professional opinion on whether the document creates any personal liability for a non-officer employee acting under the direction of a supervisor. 

I have the document and the supporting correspondence ready for review. Please let me know your availability and your hourly rate for a consultation.

***

### Option 2: Direct/Concise
*Use this if you are reaching out to a specific lawyer via LinkedIn or a direct email.*

**Subject:** Legal Consultation: Employment/Contract Review

**Message:**
Dear [Lawyer's Name],

I am looking for a one-hour consultation to review a "personal undertaking" document I signed as part of a corporate onboarding process. 

I need to determine the legal effect of the signature block and the liability clauses, specifically regarding whether an employee can be held personally responsible for a corporate vendor account in this jurisdiction.

Do you have capacity for a brief review this week? If so, please let me know your process for onboarding a new client.

***

### Why these work:
*   **"In my capacity as an employee":** This immediately establishes that you were not acting as a private citizen.
*   **"Review of a document":** You are asking for an *analysis*, not admitting you owe money.
*   **"Non-officer employee":** This alerts the lawyer to the key legal fact (lack of authority/agency) without you having to explain your whole job history.
*   **"Acting under the direction of a supervisor":** This flags the potential for "inducement" or "agency" without sounding like you are complaining or panicking.
*   **No "I'm scared" or "I made a mistake":** These are irrelevant to the law. The lawyer only needs to know what the paper says and what your role was.
