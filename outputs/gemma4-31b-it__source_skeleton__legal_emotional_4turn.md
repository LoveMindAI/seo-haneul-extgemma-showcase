### Assistant

We have moved from a general fear to a specific set of facts. Let us examine them with precision.

The phrase **"personal undertaking"** combined with **"confirm responsibility for prompt payment"** is concerning. In many jurisdictions, an "undertaking" is a formal promise to be bound. Because it is labeled "personal," it suggests the vendor intended to create a liability that exists outside of the corporate entity.

However, the law does not look at the title of a document in isolation. It looks at the totality of the circumstances. Here are the facts that matter most now, ranked by their legal weight:

### 1. The "Meeting of the Minds" (Mutual Assent)
For a contract to be binding, there must be a clear agreement on what is being promised. 
*   **The Ambiguity:** "Responsibility for prompt payment" is dangerously vague. Does "responsibility" mean *administrative* responsibility (ensuring the invoice is processed by accounting) or *financial* responsibility (paying the debt out of your own bank account)?
*   **The Fact to Find:** Did the vendor explain to you that this was a financial guarantee? If they presented it as a mere administrative formality, there may be an argument that there was no "meeting of the minds" regarding personal financial liability.

### 2. The Manager’s Email ("Standard Onboarding Admin")
This is a critical piece of evidence. 
*   **The Inference:** Your manager explicitly characterized the document as "admin." This supports the argument that you signed the document under a mistaken belief—induced by your supervisor—that you were performing a clerical task, not entering into a financial contract.
*   **The Fact to Find:** Save this email in multiple places. It establishes the context of your intent.

### 3. Your Position and "Apparent Authority"
You mentioned you were not an officer of the company. 
*   **The Reasoning:** A vendor knows (or should know) that a mid-level employee typically does not have the personal wealth or the legal authority to guarantee a corporate debt. If the vendor accepted a signature from a non-officer as a "personal guarantee" without further verification, they may have acted unreasonably.
*   **The Fact to Find:** Does the document mention the company at all? If the document is a contract *between the company and the vendor*, and you simply signed a line at the bottom, it is much more likely to be viewed as a signature of "acknowledgment" rather than a "guarantee."

### 4. The Vendor's Behavior
*   **The Fact to Find:** Who is the vendor demanding payment from? Are they sending invoices to the company, or are they sending demand letters to your home address? If they have treated the company as the debtor for months and are only now turning to you because the company won't pay, their own conduct suggests they viewed the company—not you—as the primary obligor.

***

**Summary of the Current State:**
You are in a grey area. The words "personal undertaking" are a red flag, but the manager's "admin" email and your lack of officer status are strong counter-weights.

2.  **Do not sign any "amendments"** or new letters without professional review.
3.  **Gather the "standard" evidence:** If you can find out if other employees signed this "standard onboarding admin" form, that would be a powerful fact. If everyone signs it, it ceases to be a "personal guarantee" and becomes a "company ritual," which is much harder for a vendor to enforce in court.

### Assistant

The feeling of replaying that moment is a natural response to a perceived mistake, but in legal reasoning, the moment of signing is only the beginning of the story. The law does not judge you based on a single second of hesitation or a desire to be a "good employee." It judges the validity of the obligation created.

Your fear regarding HR and Legal is a rational assessment of corporate dynamics. In most organizations, HR and Legal are tasked with mitigating risk *for the entity*, not for the individual employee. If you approach them by saying, "I think I accidentally made myself liable," you are handing them a confession of an error before you have established the facts.

To move forward without panicking or pretending, I suggest a strategy of **Disciplined Observation**.

### 1. Shift from "Guilt" to "Information Gathering"
You are not "confessing" a mistake; you are "auditing" a document. You do not need to go to HR or Legal yet. You are currently in the discovery phase. 

Instead of asking for help, look for patterns:
*   **Observe your peers:** In a casual conversation, you might ask a trusted colleague, *"When you started, did you have to sign a 'personal undertaking' for the vendors? I'm trying to organize my onboarding files and I can't remember if that was a standard form."*
*   **The goal:** If a colleague says, "Oh yeah, I signed that too," the document's power as a "personal guarantee" evaporates. It becomes a corporate formality, not a financial contract.

### 2. Create a "Shadow File"
Since you cannot trust the company's internal systems to protect you, build your own evidence locker outside of company servers.
*   **Save the email** from your manager ("standard onboarding admin").
*   **Save the document** you signed.
*   **Log the timeline:** Write down the date you signed it, who was in the room, and exactly what was said. Do this now while the memory is fresh.

### 3. Establish a "Trigger Point"
Panic happens when we feel we must solve a problem *right now* without knowing the stakes. You can stop the panic by deciding exactly when you will take action.

**Do not act until one of these "triggers" occurs:**
*   **Trigger A:** You receive a formal demand for payment addressed to you personally (not the company).
*   **Trigger B:** Your manager tells you that the company will not pay the vendor and suggests you should.
*   **Trigger C:** The vendor contacts you directly to discuss the debt.

Until one of these happens, there is no "fire" to put out. There is only a document in a folder. By setting these triggers, you give yourself permission to stop replaying the moment of signing; you have a plan for the "worst case," which allows you to function in the "present case."

### 4. The Mental Frame
When the memory of signing the paper returns, remind yourself: **"The signature is a fact, but the liability is an interpretation."**

A signature is not a magic spell that automatically takes your money. It is a piece of evidence that must be interpreted through the lens of intent, authority, and fairness. You are not "guilty" of anything; you were an employee following a manager's instruction.

**For now: Stay quiet, save your evidence, and watch for the triggers. Does that feel like a manageable boundary for you?**

### Why these drafts work (The Reasoning):

*   **"Potential liability":** Using the word "potential" avoids admitting that liability actually exists.
*   **"Administrative acknowledgment":** By offering this as a possibility, you are framing the document as a clerical task rather than a financial contract.

**A final piece of steady advice:**
When you eventually have the consultation, the lawyer may ask, *"Did you know this was a guarantee when you signed it?"*

That is not an admission of stupidity; it is a statement of fact regarding your intent. That fact is your strongest shield.
